Welcome to my final portfolio for NRC 588, taught by Professor Hongyu Zhang and TA James Higby.

When I came to university, I had a pretty extensive bucket list of classes. I unfortunately didn't get to take many of them—introductory psych and econ, an African-American studies class, or Hispanic linguistics—but I did get to check off a GIS class!

As someone who despises the false dichotomy between STEM and the humanities, often perpetuated by STEM practitioners who see the humanities as less rigorous, I loved the opportunity to combine my loves of data and graphic design to represent topics that are important to me.

This portfolio consists of three projects. The page you're on right now links to the embedded maps and describes relevant backstory and revisions for each assignment.

Los Angeles census tracts by percent Hispanic population [x]

Multiple of my other 588 assignments were on New York, because I'm a New Yorker, and I never let anyone forget it. So why represent my rival city?

Firstly, I thought it would be nice to stray from that pattern, and secondly, my roommate and best friend is, erm, whatever the Chinese version of un angelano is. I take pride in being able to describe and identify New York neighborhoods in atomic levels of detail, so it was cool to learn a little bit about what his hometown is like. We also got to bond over this map when he took my computer, scrolled around the neighborhoods, and explained their history.

I was honestly really proud of this assignment; it was one of few I got full marks on! I made a few small changes because in the original submission, the legend and description boxes overlapped with the Mapbox logo and weren't fitted to the text content. Now they are, which makes the map a bit more accessible and prettier.

Gender and accessibility of UMass bathrooms [x]

Bathrooms are a huge dictator of how people interact with their surroundings. They're such a necessary base resource for anyone in any building. For this production assignment, I wanted to look at how accessible UMass is by ADA standards and for people of different gender identities.

The actual task for this assignment was to reconfigure a base map with better symbology, and I chose this map of bathrooms by ArcGIS user mtaftferguso_UMass_Amherst. In my original submission, the link was broken, but it should work now! It is also embedded in the map page as the "before." The after features clustering, which I thought would be more useful than individual bathrooms, as there could be tons of bathrooms in a small space. The clusters are also colored according to the majority gender: yellow for female, blue for male, and pink for gender-inclusive. Mixing up the colors from their stereotypical associations was a deliberate decision. While it compromised the intuitiveness of reading the map, I felt it was necessary to challenge these visual cues given the content of the map.

After reconfiguring the base map, I made a chart displaying how ADA accessible bathrooms breaks down by gender. The only gender category that was majority accessible was gender-inclusive, which makes sense, since a common solution to both gender-inclusive and accessible bathrooms is single-stall bathrooms. It's also great to see that UMass's bathrooms are roughly evenly divided between genders overall, but some concerns still remain for me anecdotally about the gender distribution in the engineering buildings, and you can see this represented by most of the clusters in that area being blue.

While I didn't reconfigure this layer much, I did also include a tile layer by ArcGIS user gstoll-af_UMass_Amherst displaying the locations of stairs, which might affect the ease, and therefore accessibility, of getting into a building with a theoretically accessible bathroom.

Graffiti art survey and photo map [x]

This map comes from an assignment to make a Survey123 form allowing us to collect written, multiple-choice, etc. data alongside geographic data. I reimagined a photo blog called "Derailed" I had in early college. I posted cool graffiti and other art I saw (so mostly in New York and the Pioneer Valley), and took submissions from others via direct message. In this reimagining, respondents can submit work they see to a form that pinpoints the location, making it easier for others to go out and see it for themselves.

The actual survey [x] is closed to respondents besides myself, since I submitted mock data for the purposes of making the embedded map and didn't want people to interfere with that, but in practice they would of course be open to anyone. The map was created with an image-focused Instant App format. I chose a dark-themed base map and configured a heat map under clickable points for each graffiti location to mimic fireflies, so you can feel like you're roaming the streets at night looking at cool art!
